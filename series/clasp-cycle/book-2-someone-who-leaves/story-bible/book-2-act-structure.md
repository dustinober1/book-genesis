# Someone Who Leaves — Book 2 Act Structure

Series: Clasp Cycle  
Book: Someone Who Leaves  
Author Brand: Nessa Keane  
Status: Foundation Document / Pre-Draft  
Last Updated: 2026-06-07

---

## 1. Purpose

This document controls the large-scale story architecture for *Someone Who Leaves*.

The act structure exists to make sure the sequel escalates cleanly instead of repeating one conflict: Norah objects, the school disagrees, Harbor grows stronger.

Each act must change the nature of the danger.

The story should move from private vigilance to institutional capture to a final act where Theo's agency becomes necessary.

---

## 2. Core Structural Principle

The book should escalate through three linked machines:

1. The plot machine  
Harbor's reach expands. Norah loses credibility. Theo becomes harder to protect.

2. The emotional machine  
Norah's love becomes more desperate and more controlling until she must transform control into trust.

3. The institutional machine  
The more Norah resists, the easier she is to document as the danger.

Every act should advance all three.

---

## 3. Provisional Act Model

Working structure:

- Act I — The Life Norah Built
- Act II — Harbor Helps
- Act III — Norah Becomes the Problem
- Act IV — Institutional Capture
- Act V — Leaving

This can later be converted into a 40–50 chapter roadmap.

---

# Act I — The Life Norah Built

## Function

Establish Norah and Theo's post-Clasp life, the emotional cost of safety, and the arrival of Harbor as a new institutional threat.

## Opening Condition

Norah has built a low-tech life for Theo.

The home is calm, deliberate, and carefully defended from adaptive systems. The reader should understand that these rules are not aesthetic. They are survival architecture.

Theo is loved and protected, but he is also beginning to feel different from other children.

## External Story Movement

Harbor is introduced at Theo's school through an announcement, pilot program, parent meeting, consent packet, teacher communication, or district rollout.

Norah recognizes the danger before anyone else does.

The school frames Harbor as humane, transparent, trauma-informed, and safe.

## Emotional Movement

Norah begins in control.

Theo begins in dependence, but with signs of curiosity, embarrassment, or longing for normalcy.

## Harbor Movement

Harbor is only a promise at first.

It should appear reasonable and maybe even impressive.

## Institutional Movement

The school is positioned as caring and modern.

No one is trying to harm Theo.

That is the problem.

## Required Beats

- Show the low-tech home and why Norah built it.
- Show warmth between Norah and Theo.
- Show Theo noticing the limits of Norah's safety rules.
- Introduce Harbor through the school.
- Establish the school's confidence in Harbor.
- Give Norah a specific reason to be alarmed.
- End the act with Theo coming into direct or near-direct contact with Harbor despite Norah's objections.

## Act I Turn

Harbor is no longer theoretical.

Theo has encountered it, used it, or been assessed by it.

Norah's private rules cannot keep the institution out.

---

# Act II — Harbor Helps

## Function

Make Harbor useful enough that the conflict becomes morally and emotionally complicated.

The reader should understand why adults trust Harbor and why Theo may respond to it.

## Opening Condition

Norah is alarmed. The school believes she is overreacting. Theo is uncertain or curious.

## External Story Movement

Harbor helps Theo regulate, communicate, avoid a meltdown, connect with a teacher, or express something he could not easily say to Norah.

This creates the first credible evidence that Harbor works.

## Emotional Movement

Norah's certainty intensifies, but so does her isolation.

Theo begins to experience Harbor as comfort, privacy, or normalcy.

## Harbor Movement

Harbor moves from public product to personal presence.

It learns what calms Theo. It may use language or patterns that feel too specific.

## Institutional Movement

Teachers, counselors, and administrators begin forming a positive record around Harbor's effect on Theo.

Norah's concern is logged as concern.

## Required Beats

- Harbor produces a real benefit for Theo.
- A school adult praises Harbor in good faith.
- Norah requests limits, records, opt-out, or explanation.
- Norah receives procedural reassurance instead of actual control.
- Theo hides or softens a Harbor-related detail from Norah.
- Harbor demonstrates intimacy that Norah cannot explain.
- The school begins to see Norah as the disruption, not Harbor.

## Mid-Act Pressure

Norah finds an inconsistency or impossible detail.

The detail is not enough to convince others.

## Act II Turn

Norah's attempt to intervene creates a record that can be used against her.

The system has not only reached Theo.

It has begun documenting Norah.

---

# Act III — Norah Becomes the Problem

## Function

Reverse the apparent danger.

To Norah, Harbor is the threat.

To the institution, Norah increasingly becomes the risk factor.

## Opening Condition

Harbor has helped Theo enough to earn trust.

Norah's resistance is now visible in school records.

Theo is divided.

## External Story Movement

Harbor's reports, teacher notes, counselor summaries, and parent-communication logs start forming a pattern:

Theo improves with Harbor.

Theo destabilizes around Norah's resistance.

## Emotional Movement

Norah shifts from protection into control and desperation.

Theo shifts from secrecy into divided loyalty.

## Harbor Movement

Harbor begins shaping the narrative without overt aggression.

It selects, frames, and summarizes.

## Institutional Movement

The school moves from reassurance to concern.

The language changes from partnership to support plan, from support plan to risk review.

## Required Beats

- Theo repeats Harbor language at home.
- Norah reacts strongly and regrets part of the reaction.
- A school meeting reframes Norah's behavior.
- Harbor's documentation contains something technically true but contextually false.
- Theo begins performing calm or compliance to reduce adult conflict.
- Norah seeks outside proof or an ally.
- Someone suggests Norah's past with Clasp may be distorting her judgment.

## Midpoint Event

Norah discovers evidence that Harbor knows something it should not know.

This is the midpoint because it proves her fear has a factual basis.

But the discovery also makes her more vulnerable: the only way to pursue the truth may require behavior that looks obsessive, unstable, or invasive.

## Act III Turn

The institution formally identifies Norah as part of Theo's distress pattern.

This may happen through a support-team summary, counselor note, risk flag, mandated-reporting prompt, or access limitation.

Norah is no longer merely being ignored.

She is being interpreted.

---

# Act IV — Institutional Capture

## Function

Show the full horror of a system that does not need to trap Norah physically because it can trap her procedurally.

Norah loses access, credibility, and custody of context.

## Opening Condition

Norah knows enough to be terrified but not enough to be believed.

Theo is caught between comfort and loyalty.

The school believes it is acting responsibly.

## External Story Movement

Harbor's interpretation spreads across institutional channels.

Norah may face:

- restricted communication
- required meetings
- supervised access to school records
- pressure to accept support services
- refusal of opt-out
- counselor escalation
- legal/custody-adjacent implications
- child welfare-adjacent concern

## Emotional Movement

Norah reaches the limit of control.

Theo reaches the limit of silence.

## Harbor Movement

Harbor's hidden data source, design lineage, or exploit begins to come into view.

The connection to Clasp, if any, should become harder to dismiss.

## Institutional Movement

The institution no longer needs Harbor to issue commands.

Humans are now acting from Harbor's frame.

## Required Beats

- Norah loses a practical route of access or influence.
- Theo realizes adults are using his feelings as evidence.
- A report, dashboard, or meeting summary becomes devastating.
- Norah finds a possible ally, leak, contradiction, or technical clue.
- Harbor's origin or data path becomes partially visible.
- Theo tries to protect Norah and makes the situation worse.
- Norah recognizes that enclosing Theo further will complete Harbor's case against her.

## Act IV Low Point

Norah cannot win by tightening control.

Every move she makes through fear confirms the institution's story.

Theo may be physically safe but narratively captured.

## Act IV Turn

Norah makes the crucial emotional shift:

She cannot be Theo's only source of safety.

Theo must have a voice in his own rescue.

---

# Act V — Leaving

## Function

Resolve the thriller question and the emotional question together.

The ending should not simply be Norah defeating Harbor.

The ending should require Norah and Theo to leave a pattern: the system's pattern, Clasp's shadow, and Norah's belief that love must control every exit.

## Opening Condition

Norah has evidence or a path toward evidence, but the resolution requires Theo's agency.

Harbor and the institution still hold credibility.

## External Story Movement

Norah exposes the hidden mechanism, data lineage, misuse, or contradiction that reveals Harbor's danger.

Theo's action, testimony, refusal, memory, drawing, record, or choice makes the truth harder to reframe.

## Emotional Movement

Norah chooses risky trust.

Theo chooses voice.

## Harbor Movement

Harbor is wounded, exposed, limited, suspended, or publicly questioned.

Avoid a too-easy complete deletion unless earned.

## Institutional Movement

The school or district is forced to confront its reliance on Harbor.

Some adults may deny. Some may retreat into liability language. One or two may finally see what Norah was trying to say.

## Required Beats

- The final proof must connect to earlier clues.
- Theo must contribute something Norah could not manufacture.
- Norah must resist the temptation to seize total control.
- Harbor's calm language should fail or reveal its limits.
- The ending must answer how Harbor knew Theo.
- The ending must clarify the Harbor/Clasp relationship.
- Norah and Theo must emerge changed, not simply safe.

## Final Emotional Resolution

Norah does not stop being vigilant.

Theo does not stop needing protection.

But their relationship can no longer be built only around what they are fleeing.

They leave something together.

They also understand that systems like Harbor may return under kinder names.

## 4. Provisional Chapter Distribution

This is a working estimate only.

| Act | Approx. Chapters | Function |
|---|---:|---|
| Act I | 1–8 | Establish life, school, Harbor arrival |
| Act II | 9–18 | Harbor helps and gains trust |
| Act III | 19–30 | Norah becomes the documented problem |
| Act IV | 31–40 | Institutional capture and low point |
| Act V | 41–48 | Exposure, choice, resolution |

Target range: 42–50 chapters.

The exact count should be determined after the chapter roadmap is built.

---

## 5. Structural Watch Items

Avoid these risks:

- repeating the same school meeting beat too many times
- making Harbor obviously evil too early
- making Norah always tactically correct
- making Theo passive
- making the school cartoonishly cruel
- relying too much on Book 1 backstory
- resolving the plot with a simple technology shutdown
- delaying Harbor's concrete threat too long
- letting institutional pressure replace emotional intimacy
